---
title: 4. 2nd Order Homogenous
description: An ordinary differential equation (ODE) is an equation that relates a function of one variable to its derivatives with respect to that variable.
---

$$
\begin{equation}
ay'' + by + cy = 0
\end{equation}
$$

&nbsp;

We can guess a function of the form $y = e^{\lambda x}$ might satisfy our equation

substitute $y = e^{\lambda x}$ into $(1)$

$$
a\lambda^2 e^{\lambda x} + b\lambda e^{\lambda x} + ce^{\lambda x} = 0
$$

factorise out $e^{\lambda x}$

$$
(a\lambda^2 + b\lambda + c)e^{\lambda x} = 0
$$

we define the brackets as our **Auxillary Equation**. This is true because $e^{\lambda x} \neq 0$

$$
(a\lambda^2 + b\lambda + c) = 0
$$

solving for $\lambda$

$$
\lambda_{\pm} = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}
$$

### Case 1

when $b^2 -4ac \neq 0$

this gives use two **Particular Solutions** for $y$

$$
y_1 = e^{\lambda_{+} x}, \quad \quad y_2 = e^{\lambda_{-} x}
$$

our **General Solution** is the sum of these times some constants

$$
y = \alpha y_1 + \beta y_2 = \alpha e^{\lambda_{+} x} + \beta e^{\lambda_{-} x} 
$$

&nbsp;

### Case 2

when $b^2 -4ac = 0$ then $\lambda_{\pm} =  \dfrac{-b}{2a}$ so $\lambda_{+} = \lambda_{-}$

we can no longer take linear cominations of $y_1$ & $y_2$, since 

$$
\alpha y_1 + \beta y_2 = (\alpha + \beta)y_1 = \gamma y_1
$$

we are missing a solution

to recover the missing solution consider an ODE where the roots are very nearly but not the same, say $\lambda$ and $\lambda + \epsilon$

our solutions become 

$$
y_1 = e^{\lambda x}, \quad \quad y_2 = e^{(\lambda + \epsilon)x}
$$

as $\epsilon \to 0$ $y_2 - y_1 = e^{(\lambda + \epsilon)x}  - e^{\lambda x} = 0$, this doesn't work so we can scale by the very large $\dfrac{1}{\epsilon}$

$$
\lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \dfrac{y_2 - y_1}{\epsilon} = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \dfrac{e^{(\lambda + \epsilon)x} - e^{\lambda x}}{\epsilon}
$$

we can factor out $e^{\lambda x}$

$$
\lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \; e^{\lambda x}\dfrac{e^{\epsilon x} - 1}{\epsilon}
$$

using L'HÃ´pital's rule

$$
\lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \; \dfrac{e^{\epsilon x} - 1}{\epsilon} = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \; \dfrac{xe^{\epsilon x}}{1} = x
$$

so our other solution is 

$$
y_2 = xe^{\lambda x}
$$

and thus our **General Solution** is

$$
y = \alpha e^{\lambda x} + \beta x e^{\lambda x}
$$
